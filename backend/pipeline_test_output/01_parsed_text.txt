From a bathroom office to a $9 billion prediction empire
Polymarket is the most consequential prediction market ever built — a platform that went
from zero volume in June 2020 to $9 billion in 2024 trading volume,
effectively
called the US presidential election hours before networks, and attracted a $2 billion
investment from the parent company of the New York Stock Exchange.
The
story of how a 22-year-old NYU dropout built this from his bathroom-turned-office during
COVID lockdowns is one of the stranger founder narratives in crypto. It involves a $1.4
million federal fine,
an FBI raid, a mysterious French whale who bet $80 million
on Trump, and a strategic pivot that nearly killed the company before it became
unavoidable.
A college dropout, an Ethereum ICO, and a pandemic
Shayne Coplan was born in 1998
on Manhattan's Upper West Side.
At 14, he cold-emailed the SEC about creating an electronic stock exchange.
At 16, he spent $150 buying Ethereum at $0.30 per token during the 2014
ICO — reportedly one of the youngest participants.
He dropped out of NYU's
computer science program
during his freshman year,
founded a failed
DeFi project called TokenUnion, then another called Union.market.
By early
2020, he was selling personal belongings to cover rent on his Lower East Side apartment.
Then COVID hit. Coplan watched misinformation flood social media and saw an
opportunity.
Influenced by Friedrich Hayek's theories on decentralized
knowledge
and Robin Hanson's concept of "futarchy" (governance by betting
markets),
he coded Polymarket from his bathroom.
The core
thesis was simple: if people had to put real money behind their predictions about COVID
outcomes, the resulting prices would be more accurate than pundits, polls, or public health
agencies.
He had written to Hanson in early 2019 expressing his ambition to
"bring prediction markets to life." Hanson was reportedly skeptical.
The platform launched in June 2020
under the corporate name
Blockratize, Inc.
The critical early technology decisions shaped everything that
followed. Coplan deployed on Polygon (an Ethereum Layer 2) using USDC stablecoin as
the base currency,
with the Gnosis Conditional Token Framework for
market mechanics
and UMA Protocol's optimistic oracle for
decentralized resolution.
Each market posed a binary yes/no question, with
shares priced between $0 and $1
— a 70-cent "Yes" share meant a 70% implied
probability.
The Polygon/USDC combination gave Polymarket a decisive edge
over predecessors like Augur,
which required running Ethereum nodes,
paying enormous gas fees, and navigating a confusing native token system.
Polymarket charged zero trading fees from day one, a deliberate choice to
bootstrap liquidity.

COVID markets, the 2020 election, and a $4 million seed round
Polymarket's first markets were about COVID-19: vaccine timelines, case count thresholds,
lockdown durations.
One early market asked whether a federal
emergency use authorization would be granted for a COVID vaccine before 2021 — traders
correctly predicted
yes.
These markets attracted the
LessWrong rationalist community (forecaster Zvi Mowshowitz actively collaborated with
Polymarket on market design), crypto-native DeFi users comfortable with wallets and
stablecoins, and a growing audience frustrated with conflicting pandemic information.
Polymarket positioned itself as a "truth machine"
— the tagline was "trade
your beliefs."
The second wave of early traction came from the 2020 presidential election.
Trump vs. Biden markets brought political bettors and news junkies to the platform.
On November 3, 2020 (Election Day), daily trading volume hit $1.297
million
— a massive number for a platform that had existed for only five
months. Polymarket correctly signaled Biden's victory weeks before the race was officially
called,
establishing early credibility.
By October 2020, the platform had accumulated just over $1 million in cumulative trading
volume
when it closed a $4 million seed round led by Polychain Capital.
The angel roster read like a crypto all-star team: Naval Ravikant
(AngelList), Balaji Srinivasan (former Coinbase CTO),
Robert Leshner
(Compound founder), Stani Kulechov (Aave founder), Kain Warwick (Synthetix), and
Meltem Demirors (CoinShares).
The go-to-market strategy was entirely news-
cycle driven: create markets around trending events, rely on organic sharing via crypto
Twitter, and let the zero-fee model attract liquidity. The team DMed crypto influencers,
posted in subreddits, and kept the number of active markets deliberately small to
concentrate the thin user base around a handful of liquid markets.
A quietly raised $25 million Series A led by General Catalyst closed
around
December 31, 2021,
with participation from
Polychain and Airbnb co-
founder Joe Gebbia. The round was not widely publicized,
likely because by then,
federal regulators were already asking questions.
The CFTC settlement that nearly killed the platform
On January 3, 2022, the CFTC announced its first major DeFi enforcement action.
Polymarket had been offering off-exchange event-based binary options — classified as
"swaps" under the Commodity Exchange Act — without registering as a Designated
Contract Market. The settlement required a $1.4 million civil monetary penalty
(reduced due to "substantial cooperation"),
the wind-
down of all non-compliant markets,
and a cease-and-desist order. Acting
Enforcement Director Vincent McGonagle stated: "All derivatives markets must operate
within the bounds of the law regardless of the technology used."

The immediate consequence was severe: Polymarket geo-blocked all US users,
cutting off its largest market.
For a startup that had just raised $25
million to scale domestically, this was potentially fatal. The platform had offered more than
900 separate event markets since inception,
and now its core
audience was locked out. Trading volume cratered through 2022.
Coplan's response was a strategic pivot executed in three parts. First, in May 2022, he hired
former CFTC Chairman J. Christopher Giancarlo
— nicknamed "CryptoDad" —
as chairman of the advisory board,
signaling long-term intent to re-
enter the US legally.
Second, Polymarket shifted its focus to international users,
with Germany, Canada, and the UK becoming primary markets.
Third, the team
executed a critical technical migration: in late 2022, they transitioned from an Automated
Market Maker (AMM) model to a hybrid Central Limit Order Book (CLOB)
— off-
chain order matching for speed, on-chain settlement for transparency.
This upgrade attracted professional market makers, improved capital efficiency, reduced
slippage on large trades, and enabled limit orders.
It transformed Polymarket from
a DeFi novelty into something that functioned like a real exchange.
Despite all this, 2023 was a year of modest volumes. The platform averaged approximately
$6.7 million per month, closing the year at roughly $73 million in total annual volume
with about 2,300 new accounts joining monthly.
There were only about 60
active markets by mid-2023.
Compared to what was coming, this was a rounding
error.
The Titan submersible and Polymarket's first viral moment
The event that put Polymarket into mainstream consciousness for the first time was not an
election or a crypto event — it was a tragedy. When the OceanGate Titan submersible went
missing
on June 18, 2023,
Polymarket quickly launched a
market:
"Will the missing submarine be found by June 23?"
The market attracted over $300,000 in wagers
and became the site's most
popular market at the time.
When debris was discovered indicating a catastrophic implosion, a resolution dispute
erupted: did finding "pieces" count as "finding" the submarine? The dispute escalated to
UMA
token holders for a vote — a live demonstration of decentralized oracle
mechanics.
Meanwhile, a tweet criticizing the bet — "What stage of capitalism
does betting on someone else's death belong to?" — went viral with over 150,000 likes.
Polymarket's official Twitter account had been actively promoting the market
and replying to viral tweets about the search,
drawing intense criticism from
Gizmodo, Mother Jones, and others.
Google searches for "Polymarket" hit an all-time high
during this episode. It was
exactly the kind of attention that is simultaneously damaging and invaluable — the
platform became known, even if the first impression was controversial. The incident also
stress-tested the UMA dispute resolution system publicly for the first time at scale.

The 2024 election explosion, in concrete numbers
The 2024 US presidential election cycle transformed Polymarket from a niche crypto
platform into a global information utility. The numbers tell the story more clearly than any
narrative:
January 2024: $54 million monthly volume,
roughly 4,000 active traders
May 2024: $59.2 million, 12,800 traders — already a monthly record at the time
August 2024: $472.9 million from 63,616 wallets,
a 10x jump in three
months
September 2024: $503–533 million, 90,037 active users
October 2024: $2.28–2.5 billion,
over 235,000 active traders
November 2024: $2.63 billion — the all-time monthly high, with 314,500 monthly
active traders
Total 2024 trading volume reached approximately $9 billion
— a 123x increase
over 2023's $73 million.
The "Presidential Election Winner 2024"
market alone attracted $3.7 billion in wagers,
the largest prediction market
contract in history by at least an order of magnitude. Election-related markets accounted
for 76–91% of total platform volume
in October.
Several catalysts drove the acceleration. A $45 million Series B led by Founders Fund
(Peter Thiel) closed in May 2024,
with Vitalik Buterin (Ethereum's co-founder)
investing personally
— the announcement generated significant crypto media
coverage. In July 2024, Biden's withdrawal from the race caused a massive spike in
Democratic nominee markets.
That same month, Nate Silver (founder of
FiveThirtyEight and America's most famous election forecaster) joined as an advisor,
lending enormous credibility.
MoonPay integration enabled credit card
deposits, finally lowering the barrier for non-crypto users.
On August 29, 2024,
Bloomberg Terminal began displaying Polymarket election data alongside traditional
polling
— Coplan tweeted "Entering mainstream news and finance
before our eyes."
The crucial shift was mainstream media adoption. The Washington Post, Bloomberg, the
New York Times, CNN, and NBC all began routinely citing Polymarket odds as an
alternative to polls.
The Trump-vs-Harris probability chart became, in the words of
one investor, "repeated millions of times in mainstream media."
JP Morgan and
major hedge funds reportedly used Polymarket data through non-US subsidiaries.
Trump's own campaign team tracked the odds from Mar-a-Lago on election
night.

The French whale who bet $80 million on Trump
The most dramatic subplot of the 2024 election was the emergence of a mysterious trader.
In mid-October, the Wall Street Journal reported that four accounts had collectively
wagered $30 million on Trump, sparking speculation about market manipulation.
Polymarket's investigation confirmed all accounts belonged to a single French trader
— a man identified only as "Théo," described as having "extensive trading
experience and a financial services background."
Théo's strategy was sophisticated. On October 15, he contacted YouGov to commission
private polling in three swing states using the "neighbor polling" technique — asking
respondents who they thought their neighbors would vote for, which revealed a hidden
Trump advantage.
Before even seeing those results, he had already bet $30
million. After the polls confirmed his thesis, he liquidated virtually all his assets and went
all in across 11 crypto accounts,
placing hundreds of small trades to avoid
moving prices — 450+ bets in a single 10-hour period.
His total wager reached
approximately $80 million.
He held roughly 25% of all Trump Electoral
College contracts and over 40% of Trump popular vote contracts.
When Trump won, Théo's net profit was approximately $78.7–85 million,
making him the biggest winner on Polymarket's leaderboard. "I have absolutely no political
agenda," he told the Journal.
"I'm just making money."
France's
gambling regulator subsequently investigated, and Polymarket
proactively
blocked French users.
Election night and the FBI raid
On November 5, 2024, Polymarket effectively called the election. By 12:51 AM EST, the
platform indicated a 97% probability of Trump winning — hours before any major
television network made the call (roughly 6 AM).
In the final weeks, Polymarket
had consistently shown Trump at 58–62% odds while polls called it a toss-up.
A subsequent academic paper (Cutting et al., 2025) found that
Polymarket outperformed polls, particularly in swing states.
Eight days later, at 6 AM on November 13, 2024, FBI agents raided Shayne Coplan's SoHo
apartment, seizing his phone and electronic devices.
The investigation
concerned whether Polymarket had allowed US-based users to bet despite the 2022 CFTC
settlement
— VPN circumvention was widespread, and estimates
suggested roughly 55% of users were actually US-based. Coplan was not arrested
or charged.
His response on X: "new phone, who dis?"
Polymarket called it "obvious political retribution by the outgoing administration."
Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong tweeted "What the f*** is wrong with
this administration."
On July 15, 2025, both the DOJ and CFTC formally ended
their investigations
without bringing new charges.

The post-election crash was immediate — volume dropped 84% overnight, daily active
users fell 53%,
and open interest plummeted from $510 million to $120 million.
But the platform did not collapse. Sports markets emerged as the new growth
engine,
with over $700 million wagered on Super Bowl 2025 and $359 million on
Champions League.
By January 2025, monthly volume had stabilized around $1.2
billion
— still 16x higher than the pre-election baseline.
The funding trajectory accelerated through previously undisclosed rounds: $55 million at a
$350 million valuation (Blockchain Capital, late 2024) and $150 million at $1.2 billion
(Founders Fund, early 2025,
with Point72 Ventures
from Steve
Cohen
and Ribbit
Capital).
In July 2025,
Polymarket acquired QCX LLC, a CFTC-licensed exchange, for $112 million
to enable legal US market reentry.
The CFTC granted an
Amended Order of Designation in November 2025,
and US access was
unblocked on December 2, 2025
— nearly four years after the original ban.
The capstone came on October 7, 2025, when Intercontinental Exchange
(the
parent company of the New York Stock Exchange) invested up to $2 billion at a
$9 billion post-money valuation.
Total 2025 trading volume reached
approximately $21.5 billion
across 95+ million transactions,
with
monthly active traders peaking at 477,850 in October 2025.
Coplan, then 27,
appeared on Bloomberg's billionaires index
with an estimated net
worth of $1 billion based on his roughly 11% ownership stake.
The
platform that had nearly died after a $1.4 million CFTC fine was now valued more than
many traditional exchanges.
The strategic arc that made it work
Looking across the full timeline, several decisions stand out as inflection points. The
Polygon/USDC technology stack solved the UX problems that killed Augur and Omen —
near-zero gas costs, fast settlement, and intuitive dollar-denominated pricing.
The zero-fee model sacrificed revenue for years but built liquidity and user habit.
The curated market approach (Polymarket creates all markets rather than allowing
permissionless creation)
maintained quality and prevented the spam that
plagued decentralized competitors.
The AMM-to-CLOB migration in late 2022
attracted
professional market makers and enabled institutional-scale trading. And
the decision to geo-block US users and comply fully with the CFTC, rather than fight or go
fully underground, preserved optionality for the legal reentry that eventually happened.
The competitive landscape validated these choices. Augur launched earlier on Ethereum
but died from unusable UX.
PredictIt had a regulatory safe harbor but capped
traders at $850 per market and lost its CFTC no-action relief in 2022.
Kalshi took
the opposite approach — full US regulatory compliance from day one
— and
built a viable competitor,
but with lower volumes until 2025.
By 2025, Polymarket and Kalshi had formed a de facto duopoly
with
combined volume exceeding $44 billion.

Conclusion
Polymarket's trajectory reveals three non-obvious insights. First, regulatory near-death
experiences can be strategic advantages — the CFTC settlement forced an international
pivot and compliance infrastructure that ultimately enabled a larger reentry.
Second,
controversial virality works — the Titan submersible backlash, the French whale drama,
and the FBI raid all generated attention that converted into users. Third, prediction
markets are event-driven businesses with power-law dynamics — the platform did $73
million across all of 2023, then $3.7 billion on a single election question. The challenge
going forward is whether sports, crypto markets, and geopolitical events can sustain the
volume that elections create, or whether Polymarket remains a platform that explodes
every four years and treads water in between.
The $21.5 billion in 2025 volume
— driven largely by sports expansion — suggests the former, but the platform
has only existed through one full election cycle. The next test comes in 2028.
